# Who Hurts Most — AI Assignment Assistant
### Team Voke | User Research One-Pager

---

## Who They Are

African university students, mostly in their first to fourth year of undergraduate study, with a handful of postgraduate students. They are studying a wide range of courses — Software Engineering, Business Management, Nursing, Cybersecurity, Bioinformatics, Public Health, and more. They are spread across institutions in Nigeria (Miva Open University, Queens College), Ethiopia (Arbaminch University), Kenya (Maseno University, University of Nairobi), Malawi, South Africa, and Uganda. Most are digital natives but their academic tools are not keeping up with them.

---

## Where They Are

Across sub-Saharan Africa, attending a mix of physical and online universities. A notable portion are at Miva Open University — a fully online institution — which means their entire academic life happens digitally, yet they are still managing assignments manually. They rely on phones as their primary device, not laptops.

---

## How They Currently Cope

Most students are not tracking assignments at all. Of those who do, the most common tools are a paper planner or a phone calendar — neither of which was designed for academic assignment management. A few use Notion, but only a minority. Nobody mentioned a dedicated assignment tracking tool. The methods are fragmented, passive, and easy to forget.

---

## What Hurts

**1. They forget — and forget often.**
The most cited pain points are forgetting deadlines and last-minute rushing. Several students rated themselves 3 or 4 out of 5 on how often they miss or forget assessments. This is not laziness — it is a system failure. There is nothing actively holding their deadlines in front of them.

**2. Instructions are unclear and nowhere to reference.**
Multiple students flagged unclear assignment instructions as a major headache. Once a student leaves the platform where the assignment was posted, there is no single place to see what is actually required. They lose context. They second-guess themselves. They start late because they spent time just figuring out what to do.

**3. Reminders are too passive or nonexistent.**
Students are asking for reminders repeatedly — before 2 days, daily, alarm-style notifications. This tells you that the gap is not awareness of the deadline at the time it is set, but memory of it days later when life gets busy. They need something that comes to them, not something they have to go back to.

**4. No structure for breaking assignments down.**
One student asked for a tool that grows a "motivation tree" each time they complete work. Another asked for adaptive scheduling that adjusts as the day progresses. These are not feature requests — they are signals that students feel overwhelmed by assignments as single large tasks with no sense of progress.

---

## The Student Who Hurts Most

A 200-level Software Engineering student at an online university like Miva. She does not track her assignments at all. She rates herself a 3 or 4 on missing deadlines. Her biggest problem is that she rushes at the last minute — not because she does not care, but because nothing reminded her the deadline was coming until it was almost too late. She wants something that stores her assignment details, knows the rules and requirements of each task, and reminds her automatically two days before submission. She does not want to manage the tool. She wants the tool to manage the deadline for her.

---

## What This Means for the Product

Students do not need a smarter to-do list. They need a tool that:

- Holds the full context of each assignment in one place — instructions, deadline, course
- Pushes reminders to them automatically, not waiting for them to check in
- Breaks assignments into steps so the task feels manageable, not overwhelming
- Works on mobile first, because that is the device they actually use

The AI layer earns its place by doing the breakdown and the reminding intelligently — not just storing information, but anticipating when a student needs a nudge and what they need to do next.

---

*Based on 17 survey responses collected from African university students across Nigeria, Ethiopia, Kenya, Malawi, South Africa, and Uganda.*
